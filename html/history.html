The history of Asians in America and the discrimination they faced


As word of the gold mine started to spread, many people rushed to California, increasing the immigration to America. Among the people going for the gold rush were Chinese immigrants. Unfortunately, after the rise of Chinese immigrants in the US, the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) was enacted. The act made it illegal for Chinese laborers to immigrate to the US for 10 years. Nonlaborers and Chinese people renetering the US had to get a certificate from the Chinese government to immigrate to the US, which was a hard task to do as it was difficult to prove they were nonlaborers. Congress disallowed federal and state courts to grant citizenship to Chinese reisidents, but the courts can still deport them. When the act expired, the Geary Act started, which required Chinese residents to register and get a certificate of residence or else they would be deported. It also extended the exclusion for 10 years.

The Hells Canyon Massacre resulted in the deaths of 34 chinese men. Their bodies were mutilated and dumped in a river. Unfortunately, an all-white jury found the perpetrators innocent.

The popular case of Tape v. Hurley highlighted the barring of education that Asians faced in that period. Tape, a Chinese-American girl, was denied admissions to school because of her race. Her family sued and the Supreme Court ruled in their favor.

The Page Act of 1875 restricted the hiring of people from "Oriental" countries like China and Japan.

The Immigration Act of 1917 banned Chinese, Asian Indians, Burmese, Thai, Maylays and other ethinicities.
